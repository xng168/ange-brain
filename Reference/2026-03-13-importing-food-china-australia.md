## Importing Food from China into Australia

### The Two Regulatory Layers

Every food import must clear **two separate hurdles** before it can be sold in Australia:

**1. Biosecurity Requirements** (under the Biosecurity Act 2015)
**2. Food Safety Requirements** (under the Imported Food Control Act 1992)

Both are administered by the **Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Forestry (DAFF)**.

---

### Step 1 — Check BICON (Biosecurity Import Conditions System)

The BICON system is your first stop. It tells you whether your commodity can be imported into Australia, what import conditions apply, and whether you need a biosecurity import permit.

Any commodity not listed in BICON for the intended country of export **cannot be imported** until a risk assessment is conducted and import conditions are developed. This process can take several years.

**For packaged sweet potato specifically:**
- *Fresh* sweet potato from China would face very high biosecurity barriers and likely require a multi-year risk analysis.
- *Packaged/processed* sweet potato (e.g. vacuum-sealed, cooked, or dried ready-to-eat products) is treated differently. Sufficient freezing, drying, and further processing (such as skinning, peeling, cooking) can significantly reduce biosecurity risks.

---

### Step 2 — Food Safety Compliance (IFIS)

Foods imported for sale in Australia must comply with the **Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code (the Code)**. Food importers are responsible for ensuring that all food they import complies with the relevant standards.

The **Imported Food Inspection Scheme (IFIS)** inspects and tests imported food at the border. The frequency of inspection and testing depends on the food safety risk of the food imported.

---

### Step 3 — Labelling Requirements

Food imported for sale must comply with the **Country of Origin Food Labelling Information Standard** and the Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code.

This means your packaging must clearly show:
- Country of origin ("Product of China")
- Ingredients list
- Nutritional information panel
- Allergen declarations
- Best before / use by date
- Importer's Australian business name and address

---

### Step 4 — Customs & Import Declaration

You must lodge details of your food import in a **Full Import Declaration** via the Cargo Online Lodgement System (COLS).

---

### Step 5 — Tariffs & GST

Under the **China-Australia Free Trade Agreement (ChAFTA)**, most goods from China can enter Australia tariff-free, provided they meet rules of origin. Even when tariffs are waived, a **10% GST** applies, calculated on the CIF value (cost + insurance + freight).

---

### Importer Responsibilities

If you are importing food for sale, you have legal responsibilities to take steps to assure yourself that the food is **safe for human consumption** and meets Australia's biosecurity import conditions, food standards, and other import requirements.

You must also keep records of where your imports come from.

---

### Practical Checklist for Packaged Sweet Potato from China

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | Search **BICON** (bicon.agriculture.gov.au) for your specific product |
| 2 | Determine if a **biosecurity import permit** is needed |
| 3 | Confirm product complies with the **FSANZ Food Standards Code** |
| 4 | Ensure packaging meets **Australian labelling laws** (Country of Origin, ingredients, etc.) |
| 5 | Lodge a **Full Import Declaration** via COLS |
| 6 | Be prepared for border **inspection and testing** under IFIS |
| 7 | Confirm **ChAFTA eligibility** with DFAT's FTA Portal to minimise tariffs |
| 8 | Pay **10% GST** on CIF value |

---

### Key Agencies & Resources

- **BICON**: bicon.agriculture.gov.au — check import conditions
- **DAFF Imported Food**: agriculture.gov.au/biosecurity-trade/import/goods/food
- **FSANZ**: foodstandards.gov.au — food standards code
- **DFAT FTA Portal**: fta.dfat.gov.au — confirm ChAFTA tariff rates
- **DAFF Helpline**: 1800 900 090

> **Important note:** Regulations can change, and requirements vary by the exact form of the product (fresh vs. frozen vs. dried vs. cooked). Always verify current conditions in BICON before importing, and consider consulting a licensed customs broker experienced in food imports.

---

## Process 1 — Biosecurity Requirements (Biosecurity Act 2015)

This is about protecting Australia from pests, diseases, and contaminants that could harm agriculture and the environment.

### Step 1: Search BICON Before You Do Anything Else

Go to **bicon.agriculture.gov.au** and search for your product. BICON houses Australia's biosecurity import conditions for more than 20,000 plants, animals, minerals and biological products. It will tell you what biosecurity import conditions exist and whether an import permit case is required.

For packaged sweet potato from China, you'd search using descriptors like "sweet potato", "processed vegetable", or your product's HS tariff code. BICON will tell you:
- Whether the product **can** be imported at all
- What specific conditions must be met
- Whether you need an **import permit**

---

### Step 2: Determine Your Permit Type

All standard permit cases will identify whether a permit is required and list each of the conditions required. If you can meet all of the listed import conditions, you apply for a **standard permit**. If you cannot meet all conditions, you need to apply for a **non-standard permit**, which requires a more detailed biosecurity risk assessment.

**Standard permit** (simpler path):
- Costs $209 total — a $130 standard application levy plus a $79 assessment fee. Once approved, a standard permit can cover multiple goods in the same commodity class.

**Non-standard permit** (complex path):
- Required when your product doesn't fit standard conditions — this involves a detailed case-by-case risk assessment and takes significantly longer.

---

### Step 3: Gather Supporting Documents & Apply

You can apply for an import permit through BICON after registering as a user. The department will assess your application and may grant a permit subject to conditions deemed necessary for safe importation, use and disposal. Applying does not automatically result in a permit being issued.

You may need to attach documents such as:
- **Manufacturer's/Supplier's declaration** (confirming processing methods, ingredients, country of origin)
- Product specifications and ingredient lists
- Evidence the product has been treated (e.g., cooked, dried, or otherwise processed to reduce biosecurity risk)

---

### Step 4: Wait for Assessment & Decision

The service standard is that 90% of applications will be decided within **20 business days** for standard (category 1) applications and within **40 business days** for non-standard applications after receiving payment and a completed application.

> ⚠️ **Critical rule:** The permit must be granted **before the goods arrive in Australia**. Goods that arrive without a valid permit will be exported or destroyed at the importer's expense.

---

### Step 5: Comply With Permit Conditions at the Border

Once goods arrive, biosecurity officers check that they match the permit. Non-commodity biosecurity risks are also assessed at the border — including container cleanliness, packaging, and destination concerns — and goods may be subject to inspection and treatment on arrival.

---

## Process 2 — Food Safety Requirements (Imported Food Control Act 1992)

This is a separate, parallel process that runs alongside biosecurity. It's about ensuring the food is **safe to eat** and **correctly labelled** for Australian consumers.

### How IFIS Classifies Your Food

The IFIS operates based on food being classified into categories. Food is classified as **risk food** based on advice from Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ) that it poses a medium or high risk to public health. All consignments of risk food are referred for inspection, with the rate based on the food's history of compliance.

**Surveillance food** (lower risk) is randomly referred — only 5% of consignments are selected for inspection. When a surveillance food fails, a holding order is applied and future consignments are inspected at 100% until a history of compliance is established.

For packaged ready-to-eat sweet potato from China, it would likely be classified as **surveillance food** initially, though this depends on BICON's current food safety case for the product.

---

### The Inspection Process — What Actually Happens

**Stage 1: Arrival & Automatic Referral**

The Department of Home Affairs' Integrated Cargo System (ICS) refers shipments of imported food for inspection and testing. A **Food Control Certificate (FCC)** is issued to notify you that your food needs to be inspected. The food must remain on hold — you cannot distribute it.

**Stage 2: The Inspection Itself**

All food consignments inspected under IFIS have a **visual and label assessment**. Samples may also be taken for analytical tests depending on the type of food.

The label inspection checks:
- Correct country of origin labelling
- Ingredients list and allergen declarations
- Nutritional information panel
- Best before / use by dates
- Australian importer name and address

The department now offers **virtual label and visual inspections** of eligible surveillance food as a permanent option. An authorised officer connects with the importer online via Microsoft Teams to conduct the inspection remotely, reducing delays and allowing continuity of supply.

**Stage 3: Outcome**

If the inspection is only a label and visual assessment, you'll receive an **Imported Food Inspection Advice (IFIA)** at the end of the inspection. If samples were taken for testing, the IFIA is sent once testing results are known.

- ✅ **Pass** → food is released for distribution
- ❌ **Fail** → the food cannot be released. The importer must either relabel (if labelling issues were found), or destroy or re-export the food under the supervision of an authorised officer.

---

### Alternative: Food Import Compliance Agreement (FICA)

For high-volume, established importers there's an easier long-term path. A **Food Import Compliance Agreement (FICA)** offers importers an alternative regulatory arrangement — instead of border inspections, the department audits your existing documented food safety management system. Food imported under a FICA is not referred to the IFIS for inspection.
