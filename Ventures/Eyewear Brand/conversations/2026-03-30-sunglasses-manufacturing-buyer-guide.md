Here's a breakdown of what you need to know across each of those areas.

---

## 1. Frame materials

This is one of the first conversations you'll have with any manufacturer.

**Acetate** (also called zyl or cellulose acetate) is the premium standard — derived from cotton or wood pulp, it can be polished to a high gloss, takes rich colours and patterns, and has a warm, weighty feel. It's hand-finished by skilled workers and is the material of choice for fashion and luxury brands. It's also significantly more expensive and slower to produce.

**Injected plastic** (TR-90, nylon, propionate) is used for sporty, lightweight, or lower price-point frames. TR-90 in particular is extremely flexible and impact-resistant — popular for sport and kids' eyewear. These frames come off a mould rapidly, so unit costs are much lower.

**Metal** frames (stainless steel, titanium, monel, aluminium) are produced by stamping, laser-cutting, and bending. Titanium is the prestige option — hypoallergenic, extremely light, and durable, but expensive. Stainless and monel are far more common at accessible price points.

**Bio-based and recycled materials** (castor bean oil, recycled ocean plastic, reclaimed acetate) are growing in demand. Manufacturers with sustainable lines are increasingly common, especially in Guangzhou and Wenzhou, China.

---

## 2. Frame production methods

**CNC milling** is used for acetate — sheets are cut, shaped, and polished through multiple machine and hand-finishing stages. A well-made acetate frame can go through 20–30 steps.

**Injection moulding** produces TR-90 and nylon frames rapidly. Tooling (the mould) is a significant upfront cost — typically $1,500–$8,000 per mould — but per-unit costs drop dramatically at volume.

**Sheet metal stamping and laser cutting** is used for metal fronts and temples. Hinges are soldered or riveted.

Something important to ask manufacturers: what percentage of their finishing is done by hand vs machine? This directly affects quality ceiling and cost floor.

---

## 3. Hinges and hardware

Hinges are one of the most important quality indicators. Key types:

- **Barrel hinges** — the basic standard, 5-barrel being common in fashion frames
- **Spring hinges** — contain a small spring mechanism that allows the temple to flex outward; better comfort and durability
- **Hingeless or flex-frame** — the entire temple flexes, common in TR-90 sport frames
- **Screws and barrel quality** — the gauge and thread quality of tiny screws is an underrated durability factor

Ask to see hinge open/close cycle testing. A good hinge should survive 50,000+ cycles without failure. Cheap hinges are the most common cause of returns.

---

## 4. Lens materials

**CR-39 (plastic)** is the most common optical-grade lens material — lightweight, clear optics, easy to tint. Standard for non-impact eyewear.

**Polycarbonate** is impact-resistant (impact-rated for safety and sport), lighter than CR-39, but slightly lower optical clarity. Inherently blocks some UV. Mandatory for children's eyewear in many markets.

**Trivex** is a newer alternative to polycarbonate — better optics, similar impact resistance, slightly more expensive.

**Glass** lenses offer the best optical clarity and scratch resistance but are heavy and shatter — largely restricted to premium heritage or niche optical brands now.

**Nylon/Grilamid** lenses are used in high-performance sport eyewear (think ski goggles, wraparound cycling frames).

---

## 5. Lens coatings — the real differentiator

This is where margin and perceived quality are made or lost. Every lens starts as a blank and gets treated:

- **UV400 coating** — blocks UV up to 400nm wavelength. This is non-negotiable for a legitimate sunglass — verify all lenses are UV400 certified
- **Polarisation** — a filter laminated into or onto the lens that eliminates horizontal glare (essential for driving, water, snow). Adds significant cost but commands retail premium
- **Anti-reflective (AR) coating** — reduces reflections on the back surface of the lens; improves aesthetics and reduces eye fatigue
- **Mirror coating** — a semi-reflective coating applied to the front surface; purely aesthetic but very popular
- **Hydrophobic / oleophobic coating** — repels water and fingerprints; important for longevity
- **Hard coat / scratch resistance** — the base protective layer on most modern lenses

A key question for manufacturers: are your coatings applied in-house or outsourced? In-house coating facilities give better QC; outsourced coating is where consistency issues often arise.

---

## 6. Lens categories (tint levels)

In most markets (EU, Australia/NZ, Asia) lenses are classified by light transmission:

| Category | Transmission | Use case |
|---|---|---|
| Cat. 0 | 80–100% | Clear / fashion, indoor |
| Cat. 1 | 43–80% | Low sun / overcast |
| Cat. 2 | 18–43% | Medium sun |
| Cat. 3 | 8–18% | Bright sun — the standard |
| Cat. 4 | 3–8% | High altitude / extreme glare — NOT for driving |

Most fashion sunglasses are Cat. 2 or 3. Always confirm the category on any sample you receive.

---

## 7. Standards and compliance

This will come up immediately in market-entry conversations.

- **CE mark (EU)** — required for European sales, governed by EN ISO 12312-1. Covers UV protection, lens power, robustness, ignition resistance
- **FDA (USA)** — sunglasses are regulated as a medical device under 21 CFR 801; UV claims must be substantiated
- **AS/NZS 1067 (Australia/NZ)** — Australian standard, one of the strictest globally; critical since you're based in Sydney
- **ANSI Z80.3 (USA voluntary)** — common additional standard for optical quality

When meeting manufacturers, ask: *Do you have current test reports for CE and AS/NZS 1067? Can you supply us with test reports per SKU?* A credible manufacturer will have these readily available. Test reports are issued by accredited third-party labs (SGS, Intertek, Bureau Veritas are the main ones).

---

## 8. OEM vs ODM — understanding the model

**ODM (Original Design Manufacturer)** — the factory has its own designs (styles) that you choose from, put your brand on, and sell. Lower minimum order quantities, faster to market, less design cost. Most entry-level buyers start here.

**OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer)** — you bring the design, they manufacture to your spec. Full creative control but requires tooling investment and higher MOQs. This is where established brands operate.

Most manufacturers do both. A hybrid approach — starting with ODM styles while developing 1–2 OEM hero styles — is very common for new buyers.

---

## 9. MOQ, sampling, and pricing

Typical MOQs in China:

- **Stock ODM styles**: 100–300 pairs per style/colour (some factories go as low as 12–50)
- **Custom OEM with new tooling**: 500–1,200 pairs per style minimum
- **Injection-moulded (TR-90)**: often 300–500 minimum due to mould cost recovery

**Sampling process**: Expect to pay for samples ($15–$80 per piece depending on complexity). First samples (1st PP sample) show construction; final pre-production samples (PP sample or Gold Seal) should match bulk production exactly. Never approve a bulk order without a signed-off Gold Seal sample.

**Unit cost ranges** (very rough FOB China benchmarks):
- Basic injection plastic, stock style: $2–$6
- TR-90 sport, stock: $4–$9
- Acetate, ODM: $8–$20
- Acetate, custom OEM, hand-finished: $15–$40+
- Metal titanium: $20–$60+

---

## 10. Quality control

Key defect categories to inspect for:

- **Lens**: optical distortion, bubbles, incorrect tint, coating delamination, scratches
- **Frame**: flash lines (excess plastic from moulding), uneven polish, colour inconsistency, warping
- **Hinge**: misalignment, stiffness, improper spring function
- **Assembly**: temple symmetry, lens seating, screw tightness

Ask manufacturers if they accept third-party inspection (SGS, QIMA, Bureau Veritas) — a reluctant manufacturer is a red flag. Standard inspection is done at the AQL (Acceptable Quality Level) 2.5 level for general consumer goods.

---

## 11. Key manufacturing hubs

- **Wenzhou & Danyang, China** — world's largest eyewear manufacturing cluster; every price point
- **Shenzhen & Guangzhou** — stronger on sport, TR-90, and trend-led fashion
- **Sabae, Japan** — premium acetate and titanium; exceptional craft, high cost
- **Cadore / Belluno, Italy** — luxury acetate and metal; "Made in Italy" premium; minimum orders often 500+
- **Taiwan** — strong in metal and mid-tier acetate

For an Australian buyer entering the market, Wenzhou/Danyang factories with export experience and existing CE + AS/NZS compliance are the most practical starting point.

---

1. What certifications do you hold, and can I see current test reports for AS/NZS 1067?
2. What are your MOQs for stock styles vs. custom tooling?
3. What is your sampling lead time, and what do you charge per sample?
4. Are lens coatings applied in-house or outsourced?
5. What QC process runs before shipment — and do you accept third-party inspection?
6. Can you share your defect rate / return rate data?
7. What's your standard production lead time after PP sample approval?
8. Do you have other Australian clients I can speak with?

---

## On-frame branding

This is the most visible expression of your brand — what the customer sees when they pick up and wear the glasses.

### Temple (arm) branding

The temples are the primary real estate for your brand name or logo. Standard options include:

- **Laser engraving** — the most durable and premium-looking method. Clean, precise, permanent. Works beautifully on metal and acetate. Adds minimal cost ($0.10–$0.50 per unit at volume).
- **Heat stamping / foil stamping** — a metallic foil or pigmented stamp pressed into the temple surface under heat. Very common on acetate. Creates a tactile, indented logo in gold, silver, or custom colours. A classic luxury cue.
- **Screen printing / pad printing** — ink applied directly to the surface. Lower cost, good for multi-colour logos, but less durable than engraving — can fade with heavy use.
- **Metal logo plaque / inlay** — a small stamped or cast metal badge riveted or glued onto the temple. Adds a premium, jewellery-like feel. Common in mid-to-high fashion eyewear.
- **Engraved temple tip** — a logo debossed into the acetate or rubber end tip (the part that sits behind the ear). Subtle and sophisticated.

### Nose bridge and front frame

Less common but used for designer-style branding: a small logo etched onto the bridge, a branded screw head, or a decorative rivet. On metal frames, laser engraving on the bridge endpiece is a clean, understated touch.

### Lens branding

Some brands print a small logo or wordmark on the lens corner — visible when looking at the glasses but disappears when worn. This is typically done via pad printing and is mostly used for retail display recognition.

---

## Logo application methods — what to ask

When you sit down with a manufacturer, bring a clear brief on your logo. Key questions:

- What application methods do you support for our frame material?
- Can you show us samples of engraving depth and foil stamp colour options?
- What's the minimum character size for engraving legibility?
- Is there a setup/tooling fee for new logo dies or laser files? (Usually $50–$200 one-time per method)

---

## Material and colour customisation

Beyond the logo itself, the frame material choices are part of your brand identity.

**Acetate colour and pattern**: Manufacturers work from acetate "block" sheets produced by specialist Italian (Mazzucchelli) or Japanese (Takiron) suppliers. You can choose from hundreds of stock patterns — tortoise, havana, marble, solid — or commission custom patterns at significantly higher cost and MOQ (often 1,000+ sheets minimum). For a new brand, working from the manufacturer's existing colour library is the practical starting point.

**Temple tip customisation**: The rubber or acetate tip at the end of the temple can be a different colour from the main frame — a small detail that reads as intentional design. Often included as a no-cost option if the manufacturer already runs that colour.

**Hinge colour and finish**: On metal frames, the hinge can be plated in gold, rose gold, gunmetal, antique silver etc. Each finish is typically a separate electroplating run, so it affects MOQ and cost. Confirming which finishes a factory runs in-house vs outsources matters here.

**Custom lens tints**: For stock ODM styles, tints are usually chosen from a swatch book (grey, brown, green, rose, blue etc.). Getting a truly custom Pantone-matched tint is possible but adds cost and requires minimum lens volumes.

---

## Packaging — the unboxing experience

Packaging is where buyers consistently underestimate both the cost impact and the brand value. A great case and box can justify a higher retail price point.

### Cases

The case is usually the most expensive single packaging component. Main types:

- **Hard clamshell case** — hinged rigid case, usually EVA foam interior with fabric lining. The standard for mid-to-premium brands. Can be covered in PU leather, fabric, or paper wrap with your logo heat-stamped or debossed. Cost: $1.50–$5.00+ each depending on material and finish.
- **Hard zip case** — a rigid case with a zip closure. Feels sportier and more casual. Popular for active or lifestyle brands.
- **Soft pouch** — microfibre, canvas, or neoprene. Low cost ($0.30–$0.80), compact, and doubles as a cleaning cloth. Common for entry price point or eco-positioned brands.
- **Magnetic closure box-style case** — a hinged rigid case that closes with a magnet rather than a latch. Premium feel, often used by fashion-forward brands. Higher cost.

Always logo the case — debossed logo on the exterior cover, or a branded lining printed inside the lid. These are standard services from most packaging suppliers.

### Retail box

For shelf or online retail, most brands use an outer cardboard box. This typically includes:

- A custom-printed box (CMYK or spot colour, matt or gloss laminate)
- An inner tray or card holder to suspend the glasses
- Your brand story, care instructions, or model details on the interior flaps

Packaging is often sourced separately from the frames — many eyewear manufacturers don't produce their own boxes and will recommend a packaging supplier, or you can source independently. Minimum print runs for custom boxes are typically 500–1,000 units, with setup costs of $100–$300 for plates.

### Accessories inside the box

Standard inclusions most brands use:

- **Cleaning cloth** (microfibre): printed with your logo or brand pattern. Cost from $0.15 each at volume. Doubles as secondary marketing material.
- **Lens cleaning spray sachet**: low cost, adds perceived value.
- **Warranty card / brand card**: a small printed card with your brand story, care instructions, and warranty terms. Important for brand positioning and customer service.
- **Hang tag**: attached to the frame for retail floor display. Usually includes model name, lens category, UV protection rating, and RRP.

### Sustainable packaging

This is a growing conversation, especially relevant for the Australian market where consumer values around sustainability are high. Options to discuss with manufacturers:

- FSC-certified cardboard for boxes
- Kraft paper / unbleached board (natural aesthetic, lower print cost)
- Recycled or ocean-bound plastic for hard cases
- Seed paper inserts or care cards
- Soy-based inks for printing

A sustainable packaging story can be a meaningful brand differentiator without necessarily raising costs dramatically — kraft paper is often cheaper than premium laminated board.

---

## Brand model — private label, OEM, or co-brand

### Private label (ODM with your branding)

You select from the factory's existing frame designs, apply your logo and packaging, and sell under your brand. This is the fastest and lowest-cost route to market. The trade-off is that other brands may be selling the same base frame — differentiation lives entirely in your branding, packaging, and lens/colour choices.

### Full custom (OEM)

You develop original frame designs with a designer, commission tooling, and own the mould. This is where strong, defensible brand identity is built — no one else has your shapes. Expect $2,000–$10,000+ in upfront tooling per style, and MOQs of 500–1,200 pairs. Not the right first step for most new buyers, but important to plan toward.

### Hybrid approach

The most pragmatic entry for new buyers: launch with 3–5 ODM styles that fit your aesthetic, apply your full branding and packaging, and use those early sales to fund 1–2 OEM hero styles in year two. This is how many successful mid-market eyewear brands got started.

1. What logo application methods do you offer — and can I see samples of each on my frame material?
2. Is there a setup fee for logo dies, laser files, or stamp tooling?
3. Do you supply cases and packaging, or do I need to source separately?
4. Can I see your current case range with your logo applied?
5. What's the minimum order for custom-printed packaging (boxes and cloths)?
6. Do you offer FSC-certified or recycled packaging options?
7. If I want a custom acetate colour, what are the minimums and lead times?
8. Can you provide a full branded sample — frame, case, cloth, and box — before I commit to bulk?

That last one is critical. A fully assembled branded sample (sometimes called a "packaging sample" or "commercial sample") is the only way to truly evaluate how your brand will look at retail. Always request one before approving any production run.

Lens coatings are where a lot of the real quality difference between sunglasses lives — and where manufacturers can easily cut corners invisibly. Here's a deep dive.

*[used visualize:read_me]*

The best way to understand lens coatings is to see them as a stack — each layer sits on top of the last, and together they define how the lens performs, looks, and lasts. Here's how that stack is built, from the inside out.

*[used visualize:show_widget]*

Now let's go through each layer in depth — from a buyer's perspective.

---

## 1. Lens substrate — the foundation

Before any coating is applied, you start with the base lens material. This matters because coatings behave differently on different substrates, and adhesion quality depends on how well the coating chemistry matches the base.

CR-39 (plastic) is the most coating-friendly substrate — it accepts tints evenly and bonds well with hard coats. Polycarbonate is trickier: it's softer than CR-39, so a hard coat is not optional — it's essential. Glass accepts coatings very well but is rarely used in sunglasses today due to weight and safety concerns. Trivex sits between CR-39 and polycarbonate in terms of coating compatibility.

One important question for manufacturers: are your lenses pre-coated blanks sourced from a supplier, or do you coat in-house? Pre-coated blanks are common and reliable for volume production. In-house coating allows more flexibility in specification but introduces more points of quality variation.

---

## 2. Polarising filter — the glare killer

Polarisation is the most valuable functional upgrade you can offer customers, and one of the most important to understand technically.

Unpolarised sunlight reflects off horizontal surfaces (water, roads, car bonnets, wet sand) as intense horizontal glare. A polarising filter is a molecular film oriented vertically — it physically blocks horizontal light waves while allowing vertical ones through. The result is that scattered surface glare is cut while ambient light and colour are preserved. This is why polarised lenses make water look deeper and clearer, not just darker.

### How it's manufactured

There are two main production methods:

The first is lamination — a thin polarising film (usually PVA, polyvinyl alcohol) is sandwiched between two layers of lens material under heat and pressure. This is the standard method for CR-39 and polycarbonate lenses. Film quality varies enormously; higher-grade PVA film produces better glare elimination and is more durable under UV exposure.

The second is cast-in polarisation — used in higher-end nylon and some polycarbonate lenses, where the polarising element is incorporated during the casting of the lens blank itself. This is more expensive but more durable, and the lens has a more consistent optical quality.

### What to check

Ask manufacturers: what is the polarisation efficiency of your film? A quality polarised lens should achieve 99.9% polarisation efficiency. Ask to see test data. A lower-quality film might achieve 95–97% and will look noticeably inferior when tested with a polarisation tester (a simple tool you should carry to every factory visit — $15–$30 online).

Also ask: where is the polarising film sourced? Korean and Japanese PVA film (e.g. from Nitto, Sanritz) is considered premium. Chinese-manufactured film varies widely in quality.

---

## 3. Tint layer — the visual character

The tint controls how much light passes through (the lens category) and what the world looks like through the lens.

### Dip tinting vs mass tinting

Dip tinting is the traditional process for CR-39 lenses: a finished lens blank is immersed in a heated dye bath, and the dye is absorbed into the porous plastic surface. The longer the dip, the darker the tint. This allows for custom colours and gradients but is a variable process — dye concentration, temperature, and dip time must all be controlled carefully. Inconsistency between batches is a known quality challenge.

Mass tinting (also called bulk tinting or in-mass tinting) means the dye is mixed into the lens material itself during casting. The colour is throughout the lens, not just on the surface. This produces more consistent colour across batches and is not affected by scratching. Common in polycarbonate and some CR-39 lenses.

### Gradient tints

A gradient — darker at the top, lighter at the bottom — is achieved by controlling dip depth and time in dip tinting. A factory producing gradients should be able to show you consistent gradient position across a batch of lenses. Request 5–10 pairs and check that the gradient line falls at the same height on every lens.

### Colour and visual neutrality

Different tint colours affect colour perception when worn. This matters for safety (notably for driving) and for end-user experience:

Grey tints are neutral — they darken everything equally without shifting colours. The most accurate for driving and general use.

Brown/amber tints enhance contrast and warm the scene. Popular for golf, fishing, and driving. Slightly distorts colour rendering.

Green tints offer good contrast with neutral colour rendering — historically associated with Persol and Ray-Ban.

Rose/pink tints enhance depth perception and reduce eyestrain. Used in ski and snow lenses.

Blue tints are largely aesthetic — they can actually reduce contrast in bright conditions. Common in fashion sunglasses.

---

## 4. Hard coat — scratch resistance

The hard coat is a thin lacquer applied over the tinted lens (and over the polarising layer, if present) that acts as a physical barrier against abrasion. On CR-39, it's critical — uncoated CR-39 scratches easily. On polycarbonate, it's even more essential, as polycarbonate is inherently softer.

Hard coats are typically silicon-based or acrylic-based, applied by dipping, spinning, or flow coating, then cured under UV light or heat. The harder the coat, the more brittle it can become — so the best formulations balance hardness with a small amount of flexibility to prevent cracking when the lens flexes.

How to test it: drag a metal key or coin across a sample lens with moderate pressure and check the result against an uncoated lens. A well-applied hard coat will show no mark; a poorly applied one will scratch immediately. Also flex the lens slightly — a very brittle hard coat will show micro-cracks near the edge under flex.

Ask manufacturers: what is the pencil hardness rating of your hard coat? Good hard coats achieve 3H–5H on the pencil hardness scale. Ask for test documentation.

---

## 5. Anti-reflective back coat — the hidden comfort layer

This is one of the most commonly omitted coatings on budget lenses, and one of the most noticeable in use.

When light hits the back surface of a sunglass lens (light coming from behind or to the side of the wearer), it reflects off the rear surface into the eye. This creates distracting ghost images, reduced contrast, and eye fatigue — especially when driving or near water. An AR back coat is a multi-layer thin-film coating applied to the inside surface of the lens that reduces this rear-surface reflection from around 8–10% down to below 0.5%.

The coating works through thin-film interference — layers of metal oxides of precisely controlled thickness that cause reflected light waves to cancel each other out. It's the same principle as camera lens coatings.

A simple test: hold the lens up to a light source and look at the back surface. A well-AR-coated lens will show a faint, single-coloured tint (usually green, blue, or purple) rather than a bright white reflection. An uncoated back surface shows a clear white reflection.

---

## 6. Mirror coating — aesthetic and functional

Mirror coatings are a semi-reflective metallic film applied to the front outer surface of the lens. They reduce the amount of light entering the lens (useful in very high-glare environments) and create the highly visible, coloured reflection seen from outside the glasses.

Mirror coatings are applied by vacuum deposition — a physical vapour deposition (PVD) process where metal atoms are evaporated in a vacuum chamber and condense onto the lens surface. Common metals used include aluminium, titanium, chrome, and various alloys, depending on the desired reflection colour.

Flash mirror (light, subtle) vs full mirror (dense, bold) are the two commercial grades. Full mirror lenses typically see a 20–30% reduction in additional light transmission on top of the tint.

Key durability concern: mirror coatings sit on the outermost surface and are inherently susceptible to scratching and peeling, especially at the edges. Ask manufacturers: how do you apply edge protection to prevent delamination from the lens edge? And is a hydrophobic top coat applied over the mirror? (It should be — this is the outermost layer and it protects the mirror.)

---

## 7. Hydrophobic / oleophobic top coat — the outer armour

The outermost coating is a fluorine-based treatment that gives the lens surface very low surface energy. Water beads off rather than spreading into a film, and oils (fingerprints, skin sebum, sunscreen) don't bond as readily and wipe away cleanly.

This coating is sometimes called an easy-clean or anti-smudge coating. It makes a significant practical difference — a lens without it shows fingerprints and water marks prominently and is harder to clean without risk of scratching.

Test it on a sample: breathe on the lens and wipe with a dry cloth. A well-hydrophobic lens cleans in one or two light strokes. An uncoated or degraded lens will smear.

Note that this coating degrades with use — harsh cleaning products, alcohol wipes, and abrasion strip it away over time. This is expected and is not a quality defect, but worth factoring into your customer care instructions.

---

## UV400 — not a coating, but a requirement

UV protection deserves special mention because it's often misunderstood. UV400 means the lens blocks 100% of UV radiation up to 400 nanometres wavelength, covering both UVA and UVB.

For polycarbonate lenses, UV blocking is inherent in the material — the polymer itself absorbs UV. For CR-39 and glass lenses, a UV-absorbing additive must either be included in the lens material or applied as a surface treatment. A dark tint alone does NOT mean UV protection — a cheap dark lens without UV treatment is actively dangerous, because the pupil dilates in the shade of the tint while UV still passes through.

For the Australian market under AS/NZS 1067, UV400 compliance is tested and mandatory for anything labelled as sunglasses. Request spectrophotometer test reports for UV blocking from your manufacturer per SKU. This is non-negotiable.

---

## In-house vs outsourced coating — the QC question

One of the most important questions you'll ask a manufacturer: which of these coatings do you apply in-house, and which do you outsource?

In-house coating lines give the factory direct control over every step. They can test, adjust, and trace issues immediately. Outsourcing the coating — especially to smaller local shops — introduces variability in dip consistency, hard coat thickness, AR layer uniformity, and top coat application.

A tier-one manufacturer in Wenzhou or Danyang will typically have in-house vacuum coating chambers for mirror and AR coats, in-house dip tinting tanks, and in-house UV curing lines for hard coat. Ask for a factory tour specifically covering the coating department — it's one of the most revealing parts of any supplier visit.

---

## Quick reference: what to ask manufacturers about coatings

1. Which coatings do you apply in-house vs outsource?
2. Can you provide spectrophotometer UV blocking test reports per lens SKU?
3. What is the polarisation efficiency of your film, and where is the film sourced?
4. What is the pencil hardness rating of your hard coat? Can you show test data?
5. Do all lenses include an AR back coat as standard, or is it an add-on?
6. How is the hydrophobic top coat applied — is it included on mirrored lenses?
7. Can I see your coating delamination/adhesion test process?
8. For gradient tints — can you show batch consistency across 10 pairs?

Great area to go deep on — UV protection and lens categories are the two most safety-critical specifications in sunglasses, and they're the first thing regulators and compliance labs scrutinise. Here's everything you need to know as a buyer.

---

## UV protection — the science first

The sun emits ultraviolet radiation across three bands:

**UVC (100–280nm)** is almost entirely absorbed by the Earth's atmosphere and ozone layer. Irrelevant for sunglass specification under normal conditions.

**UVB (280–315nm)** is the band responsible for sunburn, cataracts, and most UV-related eye damage. Partially absorbed by atmosphere — more intense at altitude and in high-reflection environments like snow and water.

**UVA (315–380nm)** penetrates the atmosphere more completely. Associated with longer-term eye damage including macular degeneration and lens damage. Less acutely damaging than UVB but cumulative over a lifetime.

**UV400** is the consumer standard — lenses blocking all radiation up to 400nm cover the entire UVA and UVB range with a safety margin. This is what every legitimate sunglass lens should deliver.

*[used visualize:show_widget]*

That last point bears repeating in every supplier conversation: a dark grey lens that passes no UV at all is more dangerous than wearing nothing. The tint causes the pupil to dilate in the shaded environment, allowing more UV in through the sides and the unprotected areas. UV protection and tint darkness are completely independent specifications.

---

## How UV blocking is achieved

There are two mechanisms, and a good lens should use at least one:

**UV-absorbing additives in the substrate** — the lens material itself (CR-39, polycarbonate, glass) has UV-absorbing compounds mixed in during manufacturing. Polycarbonate inherently absorbs UV up to ~380nm due to its molecular structure. High-quality CR-39 blanks from reputable suppliers (e.g. PPG, Mitsui) include UV absorbers as standard. This is the most durable form of UV protection because it can't be scratched or worn off.

**UV-absorbing surface treatment / coating** — a thin coating applied to the lens surface that blocks UV. This is common on cheaper CR-39 and glass lenses where the substrate itself doesn't inherently block UV. It works, but it's a coating — meaning it can degrade with abrasion, harsh cleaning chemicals, or UV exposure over time.

The question to ask your manufacturer: is UV blocking achieved through the substrate or through a surface treatment? If surface treatment — what is the expected lifespan of the UV block under normal use conditions?

---

## How UV protection is tested and certified

UV transmission is measured using a **spectrophotometer** — an instrument that shines light of specific wavelengths through the lens and measures how much passes through. A test report will show a spectral transmission curve across the UV and visible spectrum.

For UV400 compliance, the key threshold is: **the lens must transmit less than 1% of UV radiation at all wavelengths from 280nm to 380nm, and ideally zero through 400nm**.

Under the key standards:

**EN ISO 12312-1 (EU, CE mark)** requires UV transmittance to be very low across the UV range, and also defines maximum UV transmittance relative to visible light transmittance — the idea being that UV protection should scale appropriately with how dark the lens is.

**AS/NZS 1067.1 (Australia/NZ)** has some of the world's strictest UV requirements, reflecting the elevated UV environment in the Southern Hemisphere. It requires that the solar UV transmittance factor (TSUV) is less than or equal to the solar blue-light transmittance — essentially ensuring that UV is blocked at least as well as high-energy visible light. Test reports must be from a NATA-accredited laboratory for Australian compliance.

**FDA 21 CFR 801 (USA)** does not have a mandatory UV standard for sunglasses, but if a manufacturer claims UV protection on labelling, those claims must be substantiated. ANSI Z80.3 is the voluntary standard used in practice.

When requesting test reports, ensure they show the full spectral curve (not just a pass/fail result), state the specific standard tested against, and come from an accredited third-party lab — SGS, Intertek, Bureau Veritas, or TÜV Rheinland are the main names you'll see.

## Lens categories — the full picture

Lens categories (also called filter categories or shade numbers) define how much visible light a lens transmits. They are the single most important specification for regulatory compliance and end-use suitability.

## How transmission is measured

The luminous transmittance figure (Tv) is measured using a spectrophotometer across the visible spectrum (roughly 380–780nm), weighted against the photopic luminosity function — essentially, weighted to how sensitive the human eye is to different wavelengths in daylight conditions. This weighting is standardised (CIE standard illuminant D65 for daylight, observer V(λ) function).

This matters because two lenses can look equally dark to the eye but have different measured Tv values if they transmit differently at wavelengths where the eye is more or less sensitive. A manufacturer saying "it looks like a Cat 3 to me" is meaningless — it must be measured.

Test reports will show Tv as a percentage. For your buying purposes, the key checkpoints are:

- Cat 3 must measure between 8% and 18% — anything outside that range fails
- Cat 4 must measure between 3% and 8%
- The stated category must be printed on the lens or frame and included in the product documentation

---

## Luminous transmittance vs solar transmittance — the distinction

There are actually two transmission figures that standards measure, and confusing them is a common source of errors:

**Luminous transmittance (Tv)** is the measure described above — photopically weighted visible light. This is what determines the lens category.

**Solar transmittance (Te or TSOL)** measures the total solar energy passing through the lens, weighted across the full solar spectrum including near-infrared. A lens can have a low Tv (dark, Cat 3) but a relatively higher solar transmittance if it doesn't block infrared well. This matters for comfort on hot days and is tested under AS/NZS 1067 and ISO 12312.

**Solar UV transmittance (TSUV)** is the third measure — the proportion of solar UV radiation transmitted. Under AS/NZS 1067, this is required to be less than or equal to the solar blue-light transmittance, which essentially ensures UV is attenuated at least as well as high-energy visible light. This is the tightest UV test in any major standard globally.

As a buyer, you don't need to compute these yourself — but when you receive a test report, you should be able to locate all three figures and confirm they fall within the correct ranges for your target market.

---

## The driving suitability requirement — critical for your labelling

This is one of the most legally significant aspects of lens specification, especially in Australia.

Under AS/NZS 1067, lenses must be tested for **traffic signal recognition** — the ability to correctly identify the colours of road traffic signals (red, amber, green, blue) through the lens. This is a colorimetric test using CIE standard observer functions and standardised signal colours. A lens that passes is deemed suitable for driving. A lens that fails, or that is a Cat 4 (too dark regardless of colour rendering), must be labelled "not suitable for driving and road use."

This is not optional labelling — it's a mandatory requirement and a genuine safety specification. A buyer selling Cat 4 lenses without the correct driving warning label in Australia faces potential liability and regulatory action.

The practical implication for buyers: if you're building a lifestyle fashion brand, most of your range will be Cat 2–3 and driving-safe. If you include any very dark or mirror lenses, request explicit traffic signal recognition test results and check whether they pass.

---

## How mirror coatings affect the measured category

This trips up many new buyers. A mirror coating sits on the outside of the lens and reflects a portion of incoming light before it even reaches the tinted lens. This means a mirror-coated lens will measure a lower Tv than the same lens without the mirror — the combined effect of tint plus mirror can push a lens that would otherwise be Cat 3 into Cat 4 territory.

The consequence: a mirrored lens must be tested as a finished product — tint plus mirror coating — to establish the actual category. You cannot determine category from the tint alone when a mirror is present. Test your final production samples in the exact finished state before committing to labelling.

---

## AS/NZS 1067 specifics for Australian buyers

Since you're based in Sydney and will be selling into the Australian market, AS/NZS 1067 is your primary compliance framework. Some specific points that go beyond the EU/ISO baseline:

The standard has two parts: AS/NZS 1067.1 covers sunglasses and fashion spectacles; AS/NZS 1067.2 covers occupational eye protection (less relevant for consumer eyewear).

Under 1067.1, the **solar UV transmittance factor** requirement is notably stricter than EU standards. The standard uses a different solar irradiance weighting (based on Australian solar conditions, which are more intense than European baselines) to calculate TSUV. A lens that passes EN ISO 12312 in Europe may not automatically pass AS/NZS 1067 — you should always test specifically against the Australian standard rather than assuming CE certification transfers.

Labelling requirements under AS/NZS 1067 include: the lens category marked on the product or packaging, whether the lens is suitable for driving, UV protection level, and manufacturer/importer identification. The standard specifies exact label language.

Test reports must come from a **NATA-accredited laboratory** for formal compliance in Australia (NATAS is the National Association of Testing Authorities). SGS, Intertek, and Bureau Veritas all have NATA-accredited facilities in Australia and in China. When requesting test reports from Chinese manufacturers, confirm the lab is NATA-accredited — not just ISO 17025 accredited — for Australian market entry.

---

## The counterfeit and substandard lens problem

This is worth raising explicitly. A significant proportion of sunglasses on the market — including from apparently credible-looking suppliers — carry UV400 and CE markings that are printed rather than tested. The markings are applied to packaging and lenses without any laboratory verification.

As a buyer, your protection is test reports from accredited labs, not markings on product. Request a test report for every lens SKU you plan to buy, from the specific production batch or at minimum from the same tooling and coating process. For a high-volume initial order, consider commissioning your own independent test through SGS or Intertek — a spectrophotometer test of a sunglass lens costs roughly $80–$150 per sample, and it's cheap insurance against a regulatory issue or a product recall.

---

## Quick reference: questions to ask manufacturers on UV and categories

1. For each lens SKU: can you provide a current spectrophotometer test report showing UV transmittance curve and Tv measurement?
2. Is UV blocking achieved through the lens substrate or a surface treatment?
3. Are your test reports from NATA-accredited labs? (For Australian market)
4. For any mirrored lenses: is the Tv measured with the mirror coating applied, or on the bare tinted lens?
5. Do any of your lenses fall into Cat 4? If so, are they labelled "not suitable for driving"?
6. Can you provide traffic signal recognition test results for Cat 2 and Cat 3 lenses?
7. How do you handle batch-to-batch consistency in tint depth — is Tv measured on every production run?

## Part 1 — Base substrates

### CR-39 (Columbia Resin #39)

CR-39 is a thermoset plastic developed in the 1940s and still the most widely used optical lens material globally. Its name comes from the 39th formulation tested by the PPG chemical group.

It is produced by casting liquid monomer into a mould and curing it — a slow process that produces a dense, optically excellent blank. The Abbe value (a measure of chromatic aberration — colour fringing at the edges of the visual field) is around 58, which is the best of any plastic lens. In practical terms, CR-39 produces very natural, undistorted vision.

Pros: outstanding optical clarity, easy to tint (accepts dye evenly), easy to add coatings, relatively low cost, good chemical resistance, lightweight (density ~1.32 g/cm³).

Cons: lower impact resistance than polycarbonate (not suitable for sports or safety eyewear without additional treatment), softer surface requiring a hard coat, slightly thicker than high-index alternatives for strong prescriptions.

Typical uses: fashion sunglasses, plano (non-prescription) sunglasses, everyday optical lenses. The workhorse material of the industry.

Chinese factory term: CR-39镜片 (CR-39 jìng piàn). Also commonly called 树脂镜片 (shùzhī jìng piàn) — "resin lens."

---

### Polycarbonate (PC)

Polycarbonate is a thermoplastic — rather than being cast, it is injection-moulded from pellets at high temperature and pressure. It was originally developed for aerospace and safety applications. Its defining characteristic is impact resistance: it meets ANSI Z87.1 ballistic standards and is the material mandated for children's eyewear in many markets.

The Abbe value of PC is around 30 — significantly lower than CR-39. This means more colour fringing, especially in wide-angle or strong-prescription lenses. For plano (zero-power) sunglasses, this difference is minimal and largely imperceptible. For prescription lenses, it becomes more noticeable.

PC also inherently blocks UV up to approximately 385nm due to its molecular structure, which is a genuine advantage.

Pros: very high impact resistance, lightweight (density ~1.20 g/cm³, lighter than CR-39), inherent UV protection, fast production via injection moulding (lower unit costs at volume), thin for given strength.

Cons: lower optical clarity than CR-39 (higher chromatic aberration), softer surface requiring a mandatory hard coat, not suitable for chemical solvents, injection moulding requires tooling investment.

Typical uses: sport sunglasses, safety eyewear, children's eyewear, budget fashion sunglasses, any application where impact resistance matters.

Chinese factory term: PC镜片 (PC jìng piàn) or 聚碳酸酯镜片 (jù tàn suān zhǐ jìng piàn).

---

### TAC (Triacetate Cellulose)

TAC is the most important material specifically for polarised sunglasses, and one that many buyers don't fully understand. It is not a lens substrate in the traditional sense — it is a laminated construction.

A TAC lens is built as a sandwich: a polarising PVA (polyvinyl alcohol) film is laminated between two or more layers of cellulose triacetate film, then hard-coated on both surfaces. The whole assembly functions as the lens. It typically ranges from 0.8mm to 1.1mm in thickness.

TAC is the dominant material for injection-moulded polarised fashion sunglass lenses because it is inexpensive to produce, accepts coatings and tints well, and the polarisation is consistent across the lens since the PVA film is laminated uniformly. The vast majority of polarised fashion sunglasses sold globally — including many well-known brands — use TAC lenses.

Pros: low cost, excellent polarisation efficiency (the PVA film is reliable), good tint acceptance, lightweight, good for flat and lightly curved lens shapes.

Cons: lower optical quality than CR-39 or PC for optics (Abbe value ~40), not suitable for highly curved or wrap frames (the laminate can crack on tight bends), softer than PC, not impact-rated, the laminate layers can delaminate if exposed to prolonged heat, moisture, or solvents. TAC lenses should not be used with certain cleaning chemicals.

Typical uses: fashion polarised sunglasses at mid and entry price points. The dominant choice for polarised eyewear globally under ~$200 retail.

Key question to ask: what is the PVA film source and what is the polarisation efficiency rating? Better TAC lenses use Japanese-sourced PVA film.

Chinese factory term: TAC镜片 (TAC jìng piàn) or 醋酸纤维素镜片.

---

### Nylon / Grilamid (TR-90 and variants)

Nylon-based lens materials are a family of polyamide plastics engineered specifically for optical and sport applications. Grilamid is a Swiss trade name (from EMS-Chemie) for a specific nylon-12 formulation that has become the industry standard for premium sport lenses and frames. TR-90 is a similar nylon copolymer widely used in Asia.

These materials are injection-moulded and produce lenses with excellent flexibility, chemical resistance, and thermal stability. They maintain their shape across a wide temperature range — from alpine cold to desert heat — which makes them the choice for serious outdoor and sport eyewear.

Pros: very lightweight, excellent flexibility and impact resistance, outstanding thermal and chemical resistance (resistant to sweat, sunscreen, and cleaning agents), good optical clarity for an injection-moulded material, compatible with highly curved wrap frames.

Cons: higher cost than PC, more limited colour options in tinting, harder to achieve the same optical benchmark as CR-39, requires careful hard-coat adhesion treatment.

Typical uses: premium sport sunglasses (cycling, ski, running, watersports), military and safety eyewear, outdoor performance brands.

Brands built on nylon lenses: Oakley (Plutonite is their proprietary PC, but competitors use Grilamid), Rudy Project, Smith Optics.

Chinese factory term: 尼龙镜片 (nílóng jìng piàn) or TR-90镜片.

---

### Trivex

Trivex was developed by PPG Industries (the same company behind CR-39) and launched in 2001. It was specifically designed to combine the optical quality of CR-39 with the impact resistance of polycarbonate, addressing the key weakness of both.

The Abbe value of Trivex is approximately 45 — better than polycarbonate (30) but lower than CR-39 (58). Impact resistance is comparable to or slightly exceeding polycarbonate. It is also inherently UV-blocking and very lightweight — actually lighter than polycarbonate at around 1.11 g/cm³, making it the lightest optical plastic in common use.

Pros: best balance of optical quality + impact resistance in the market, lightest standard optical plastic, inherent UV blocking, easy to drill (for rimless frames), chemical resistance.

Cons: higher raw material cost than CR-39 or PC, thicker than high-index materials for the same prescription power, not as widely available from Chinese manufacturers (more common from Japanese and Taiwanese suppliers), harder to find in fashion sunglass production.

Typical uses: prescription safety eyewear, rimless optical frames, children's prescription lenses, sport prescription lenses. Less common in plano sunglasses due to cost.

Chinese factory term: 特力克斯镜片 (Tèlìkèsī jìng piàn) — though you will more often need to specify by supplier name.

---

### Mineral glass

Optical glass lenses are made from silica-based glass with various mineral additives. They were the only lens material available until plastic emerged in the 1960s and were the standard for precision optics for decades.

The optical properties of glass are genuinely superior to all plastics: Abbe values of 58–60, exceptional scratch resistance (glass is intrinsically hard — no hard coat required), and near-perfect colour neutrality. A glass lens also holds its surface quality indefinitely, whereas all plastic coatings eventually degrade.

Pros: best optical clarity of any material, exceptional scratch resistance, does not yellow or degrade with UV exposure, chemical resistance.

Cons: heavy (density ~2.5 g/cm³ — roughly twice the weight of polycarbonate), shatters on impact (significant safety hazard), higher manufacturing cost, more difficult to shape for complex curves, limited relevance for modern fashion or sport frames.

Typical uses: premium heritage brands (certain Persol models, traditional craft frames), speciality optical applications, high-end photography and instrumentation. Essentially niche in contemporary sunglass manufacturing.

Chinese factory term: 玻璃镜片 (bōlí jìng piàn).

---

### High-index materials (1.60 / 1.67 / 1.74)

High-index refers not to a single material but to a family of advanced plastic formulations engineered to bend light more efficiently — requiring less material thickness to achieve the same optical power. The refractive index number (1.60, 1.67, 1.74) indicates how efficiently the material bends light. Standard CR-39 has an index of 1.50; standard PC is 1.586.

These materials matter primarily for prescription lenses — a strong minus prescription in CR-39 produces thick, heavy lens edges ("Coke bottle" effect), whereas the same prescription in a 1.74 index material produces a dramatically thinner, lighter result.

For plano (zero-power) sunglasses, high-index materials offer no meaningful optical benefit and add cost, so they are rarely used. For prescription sunglasses — a growing and lucrative category — high-index is important to understand.

Pros: dramatically thinner and lighter lenses for strong prescriptions, cosmetically superior, enables use in slim or fashion frames.

Cons: higher material cost (increases significantly at 1.67 and 1.74), lower Abbe values than CR-39 (more chromatic aberration at the edges), more reflective surfaces requiring AR coating, more brittle than CR-39 at 1.74.

Common high-index materials include MR-8 (1.60), MR-10 (1.67), and MR-174 (1.74), all produced primarily by Mitsui Chemicals in Japan.

## Part 2 — Functional treatments

These are applied to or built into base substrates. They define what the lens does, not what it's made of.

### Polarised lenses

Covered in depth in the lens coatings section, but worth summarising here: a polarising filter (PVA film) blocks horizontal light waves that create glare from reflective surfaces. It can be laminated into a TAC construction, sandwiched into a PC lens, or cast into a nylon lens blank.

The key variable between manufacturers is the PVA film quality and the polarisation efficiency (should be 99.9%+ for a quality lens). Ask to see test data. The easiest in-field test is to hold two polarised lenses face-to-face and rotate one 90° — they should blackout completely. Partial blackout means lower efficiency film.

A note for buyers: "polarised" has become a marketing term that is sometimes abused. A lens can be partially polarised, or the polarising film can degrade quickly with heat exposure. Request long-term durability test data alongside polarisation efficiency.

---

### Blue light blocking (HEV filtering)

Blue light blocking has become one of the fastest-growing lens categories globally, driven by screen time concerns. It is important to understand what it actually is, because the science is more nuanced than the marketing suggests.

High-energy visible (HEV) light refers to the blue-violet portion of the visible spectrum, roughly 380–450nm. Digital screens, LED lighting, and the sun all emit significant amounts of this light. The claimed benefits of blocking HEV light include reduced eyestrain, better sleep (by reducing suppression of melatonin production), and potential long-term retinal protection.

The scientific consensus on blue light blocking lenses is that the evidence for reducing eyestrain is weak — most eyestrain from screens is caused by reduced blink rate and focusing effort, not by blue light specifically. However, the sleep disruption effect of blue light in the evening hours is better supported, and there is ongoing research into long-term retinal effects.

For buyers, the practical reality is that consumer demand is high and growing, particularly in the computer glasses and indoor eyewear category.

How it's achieved: either through a specialised coating applied to the lens surface that reflects blue-violet light, or through a blue-absorbing tint built into the lens material. Coating-based solutions produce a visible blue/violet reflection off the front surface (which some wearers find cosmetically off-putting). Tint-based solutions produce a mild yellow cast to the lens (which affects colour rendering).

The percentage of blue light blocked varies enormously between products — from 10% to 90%+ depending on the approach. There is no universal standard defining what constitutes a "blue light blocking" lens, which creates significant potential for overclaiming.

What to ask manufacturers: what percentage of blue light is blocked (at what specific wavelength range)? Is this a coating or a substrate treatment? Do you have test reports? Is there any colour distortion, and what is the Tv impact?

---

### Photochromic / transition lenses

Photochromic lenses contain light-sensitive molecules (most commonly silver halide compounds in glass, or organic photochromic dyes in plastic) that darken when exposed to UV radiation and clear when UV is absent. They are most commonly known by the brand name Transitions (owned by Essilor).

When UV hits the lens, the molecules undergo a structural change that causes them to absorb visible light — the lens darkens. Remove the UV source and the molecules revert, clearing the lens over 2–5 minutes.

An important limitation: because the activation mechanism is UV light, photochromic lenses do not darken inside cars. Modern car windscreens block most UV, so the lenses receive insufficient UV radiation to activate. This is a common consumer complaint and worth knowing as a buyer if you're marketing these lenses.

Pros: convenience (one lens for all light conditions), good for optical frames that double as occasional sun protection.

Cons: do not work inside cars, slower to clear in cold temperatures, clear state retains a faint tint, activation speed degrades over years of use, higher cost than fixed tints.

Typical uses: optical prescription lenses, lifestyle eyewear. Less common in dedicated fashion sunglasses.

---

### Mirror coated lenses

A metallic vacuum-deposited layer on the front surface of the lens that reflects a portion of incoming light. The substrate can be any of the base materials above. The mirror effect is purely aesthetic in most fashion applications but also reduces light transmission in high-intensity environments (ski, alpine, water).

Flash mirror (subtle, semi-transparent) vs full mirror (dense, opaque-looking) is the main commercial distinction. Colour is determined by the specific metal oxide used in the deposition process — gold, silver, blue, red, green, and mercury (a rainbow/colourful mirror) are all standard options from most Chinese coating facilities.

Key durability concern: mirror coatings are the outermost surface layer (below the hydrophobic top coat) and are the most vulnerable to damage. A quality mirror lens will have a hydrophobic over-coat and will be edge-sealed to prevent delamination. Ask specifically about edge treatment.

---

### Gradient tint lenses

A gradient is a lens where the tint transitions from dark at the top to lighter or clear at the bottom. Achieved through controlled dip-tinting — the top of the lens is submerged longer in the dye bath. Double gradients (dark top, dark bottom, lighter centre) are also possible.

Pros: fashionable, allows better colour rendering at the bottom of the lens (useful for reading menus, instruments).

Cons: more production variability than flat tints, consistency across a batch needs active QC (gradient line position must be consistent across all units in a shipment). The bottom of the lens provides less sun protection.

Note: gradient lenses affect the measured lens category differently across the lens surface — the top may be Cat 3 while the bottom is Cat 1 or 2. Standards and labelling should reflect the strongest tint zone. Ask your manufacturer how they categorise and label gradient lenses.

---

### Prescription (Rx) sunglass lenses

This is a growing and profitable category for buyers to understand, even if you start with plano (non-prescription) only.

Prescription sunglass lenses combine vision correction with sun protection. The base curve of the lens must accommodate both the optical power and the frame's wrap angle — highly curved sport frames in particular are technically challenging for Rx production. Surfacing (grinding the lens to the correct prescription) is typically done by an optical lab rather than the sunglass manufacturer.

Lenses designed to be "Rx-able" (suitable for prescription surfacing) must be supplied as non-finished blanks with sufficient thickness to accommodate the grinding. Not all plano sunglass lenses are manufactured with Rx surfacing in mind — check with manufacturers if this matters for your category.

Yes — and in fact nylon (Grilamid/TR-90) produces some of the best polarised lenses available. Here's how it works and why it's used in premium sport eyewear.

## How nylon lenses are polarised

There are two main methods used for nylon polarised lenses:

**In-mould lamination** is the premium approach. A pre-formed polarising film (PVA sandwiched between protective layers) is placed inside the injection mould before the nylon is injected. The molten nylon bonds around and behind the film during the moulding process, encapsulating it within the lens. The polarising layer ends up fully integrated into the lens structure rather than sitting as a surface coating. This is the most durable form of polarisation — the film cannot delaminate, peel, or be scratched off because it's inside the lens.

**Polarising film lamination** is the alternative, where the polarising film is adhered to the front or rear surface of an already-moulded nylon lens blank under heat and pressure. Less expensive than in-mould, but the film sits closer to the surface and is more vulnerable to delamination over time, particularly with exposure to heat and solvents like sunscreen.

## Why nylon is actually superior to TAC for polarised lenses

This is an important distinction to understand when talking to manufacturers and when positioning product.

TAC, as covered earlier, is essentially a polarising sandwich by construction — the PVA film is the reason TAC exists. It's the standard for affordable polarised fashion sunglasses. But TAC has real limitations: it's softer, it can crack on tight curves, and it delaminate with heat or solvents.

Nylon overcomes all of those weaknesses. Because nylon is injection-moulded as a hard, chemically resistant substrate, a nylon polarised lens can be made in highly wrapped geometries (8-base curve and beyond), survives contact with sunscreen and sweat, and maintains its shape across extreme temperatures — from -20°C ski days to 50°C car dashboards. This is precisely why Oakley, Rudy Project, Smith, Maui Jim, and Bolle all build their sport-performance polarised lenses on nylon or proprietary nylon-variant substrates rather than TAC.

## Polarisation quality in nylon vs TAC

The polarisation efficiency of a well-made nylon in-mould polarised lens is comparable to the best TAC lenses — both can achieve 99.9%+ efficiency. The difference is durability and optical geometry, not the polarisation itself.

One optical advantage nylon has: because the moulding process can be controlled very precisely, the polarising axis alignment can be held to tighter tolerances across a curved lens. On a TAC lens, the flat film must be pressed into a curved shape, which can introduce slight distortion in the polarising axis at the edges of a strongly curved lens.

## What to ask manufacturers

When you're meeting factories and evaluating nylon polarised lenses, ask:

1. Is the polarisation in-mould or surface laminated?
2. What is the PVA film source — Japanese (Nitto, Sanritz) or domestic Chinese?
3. What base curve is the lens moulded in — can it accommodate wrap frames?
4. Can you provide a polarisation efficiency test report?
5. What delamination resistance testing has been done (heat, UV, solvent exposure)?

In-mould nylon polarised lenses are a genuine quality differentiator and justify a higher retail price point — it's worth being specific about this in your product descriptions and marketing rather than just saying "polarised."
