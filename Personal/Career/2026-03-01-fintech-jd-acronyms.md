Here's a comprehensive overview of where things stand with Australia's AML reforms.

---

**The Big Picture**

On 29 November 2024, Parliament passed the AML/CTF Amendment Bill 2024, amending the Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorism Financing Act 2006. The new laws simplify and modernise the AML/CTF regime and bring Australia in line with international standards set by the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) — the global financial crime watchdog.

This is described as the most significant overhaul of Australia's AML laws in nearly two decades.

---

**What's Actually Changing**

The reform has two major components:

**1. Changes for existing reporting entities** (banks, fintechs, payment providers, etc.) — these businesses already had AML obligations but now face significantly updated requirements. A major update to the AML/CTF program requirements shifts the focus from a compliance-based approach to a risk-based, outcomes-oriented approach. Boards and senior management are now explicitly responsible for overseeing compliance.

**2. "Tranche 2" — the big expansion.** The reforms expand AML/CTF compliance to high-risk professions like lawyers, conveyancers, accountants, real estate professionals, and precious stone dealers. This is a huge deal — these sectors have never had formal AML obligations before.

The amendments are likely to result in approximately 90,000 new reporting entities. That's an enormous number of businesses that need to get compliant.

---

**Key Dates to Know**

This is where it gets very relevant to the startup's timing:

- **31 March 2026** — Changes to obligations for current reporting entities take effect. New entities can begin enrolling with AUSTRAC.
- **1 July 2026** — AML/CTF obligations formally commence for all Tranche 2 entities (accountants, lawyers, real estate agents, etc.).

So the financial year deadline referenced in the job description likely refers to the **31 March 2026** milestone, which is now very close — less than a month away from today's date of 1 March 2026.

---

**Why This is a Massive Opportunity for the Startup**

The product is targeting NBFIs, fintechs, credit providers, and super-related entities — all of whom are either already reporting entities facing updated obligations, or newly captured under Tranche 2. AUSTRAC now has the authority to issue civil penalty orders to entities that fail to comply with their AML/CTF obligations, meaning businesses can face substantial fines if they do not adhere to the required standards.

This combination of a hard compliance deadline, expanded scope, significant penalties, and ~90,000 newly regulated businesses creates a very real and urgent market for a GRC automation tool. The burning platform is real and the window for signing up early customers is right now, as businesses are scrambling to get their programs in place before the deadlines hit.

The IPA connection also makes even more sense in this context — accountants are themselves Tranche 2 entities who need to comply, and they also advise clients who need to comply. The product could serve both the accountant and their clients.

This is a great question for understanding what the role actually involves day-to-day. Let me break it down comprehensively across the key responsibility areas.

---

**GTM Strategy — Typical Tasks**

The GTM strategy work is largely foundational and analytical at the start, then becomes increasingly executional. In practice this means:

Building out the Ideal Customer Profile in detail — not just "NBFIs and fintechs" but getting specific about employee count, revenue size, regulatory status, tech maturity, and who inside the business owns compliance. The ICP drives every other decision.

Defining the value proposition per segment, because an NBFI and a fintech have different pain points even if the product is the same. The NBFI might care most about avoiding AUSTRAC fines; the fintech might care more about scaling compliance without hiring headcount. These need to be articulated as distinct messaging frameworks.

Competitive and market mapping — understanding what else is in the Australian regtech space, how incumbents are positioned, where the whitespace is, and how to price and position against them. In a market like this, the competition is often spreadsheets and manual processes rather than a direct software competitor.

Pricing and packaging input — the JD mentions a pricing model exists but input is welcome. This involves modelling out whether a per-seat, per-entity, or usage-based model makes most sense for the target segments, and stress-testing it against buyer budget cycles in financial services.

Building the sales playbook — this is the documented, repeatable process for how a prospect moves from first contact to signed pilot. It includes talk tracks, objection handling, demo scripts, and proposal templates. Without this, early sales are ad hoc and hard to replicate.

Pipeline architecture — defining the stages, entry and exit criteria, and forecast methodology so the business has visibility on what's coming and when.

Analyst and PR engagement — getting the product on the radar of regtech analysts and trade journalists, which creates inbound credibility that supports outbound selling.

---

**IPA Engagement — Typical Tasks**

Engaging a professional body like the IPA is a partnership play as much as a sales play. It requires a different approach to a direct customer outreach. The typical tasks here would be:

Identifying the right internal contacts at the IPA — usually a Head of Partnerships, Member Engagement Director, or CEO for a body of the IPA's size. LinkedIn and the IPA's own website will surface these quickly.

Preparing a partnership value proposition — articulating specifically what value the product brings to IPA members, and equally what value the relationship brings to the IPA itself (member benefit, CPD relevance, thought leadership association). The IPA has to be able to justify the relationship to its members.

Requesting an introductory meeting framed around member benefit, not a sales pitch. The opening conversation should be about the AML challenge their members are facing and how the two organisations could collaborate, not about the product.

Exploring partnership structures — this could range from a simple referral arrangement, to co-branded educational content, to being listed as an approved technology partner in IPA communications, to co-hosting webinars for members. Each has different commercial implications and effort levels.

Developing co-branded content — whitepapers, checklists, webinars on the AML law changes that provide genuine education while positioning the product as the natural solution. This is the highest-leverage IPA activity because it reaches the entire membership rather than one contact at a time.

Speaking at IPA events — the annual conference and regional events are attended by the exact accountants who advise the target customers. A speaking slot on AML compliance is both educational and a product visibility moment.

---

**Direct Outreach to Target Customers — Typical Tasks**

This is where the BD execution happens day-to-day. For a fractional GTM lead the typical tasks are:

**Research and list building** — identifying 20–50 target accounts per segment using tools like LinkedIn Sales Navigator, ASIC's register, APRA's list of regulated entities, and commercial databases. Each account should be scored against the ICP before outreach begins.

**Contact mapping** — within each target account, identifying 2–3 contacts across different functions. For a compliance product the primary buyer is usually the Head of Compliance or Chief Risk Officer, but the CFO and CEO may also be involved in the decision. Finding all three means you have multiple paths into the account.

**Personalised outreach sequences** — writing and sending outreach that references the specific regulatory deadline, the prospect's industry segment, and a relevant insight rather than a generic product pitch. A good opening message for this context might reference the March 2026 AUSTRAC deadline and ask whether they have their AML program in place. The message opens a conversation rather than pitching a product.

**Follow-up cadence management** — a single email is rarely enough. A structured cadence across email and LinkedIn over 2–3 weeks, with different angles in each touchpoint, significantly improves response rates. This needs to be tracked in a CRM.

**Discovery calls** — once a prospect responds, the first call is not a demo but a discovery. The goal is to understand their current compliance setup, what's driving urgency, who else is involved in the decision, and what success looks like for them. This informs the subsequent demo and proposal.

**Pilot / proof-of-value proposals** — at this stage of the company's development, the ask should be a low-commitment pilot rather than a full contract. A time-limited, scoped engagement that proves value before a full commercial commitment dramatically shortens sales cycles and reduces buyer risk perception.

**CRM hygiene** — logging every interaction, updating deal stages, and maintaining forecast accuracy so the business has a clear view of pipeline health at all times.

---

**Measurement — What a GTM Lead is Accountable For**

At an early stage startup, the metrics typically tracked are: number of qualified conversations per week, number of pilots initiated, conversion rate from conversation to pilot, time to first paid customer, and net revenue retention once pilots convert. The fractional nature of this role means output focus is critical — every hour needs to move the pipeline forward.
