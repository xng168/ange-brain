Here are the key considerations for performing lip filler treatment on a gummy smile:

**Patient Selection & Etiology**

Lip filler is typically appropriate when the gummy smile is caused by soft tissue-based factors, such as a short or hypermobile upper lip. For skeletal or dental origins (e.g. vertical maxillary excess, altered tooth eruption), fillers may offer only limited improvement. Identifying the root cause first is essential.

**Anatomy & Injection Technique**

A precise understanding of upper lip anatomy is essential. One of the key muscles involved is the levator labii superioris alaeque nasi (LLSAN), which contributes to excessive upper lip elevation during smiling. The primary focus is on the vermilion border and body of the upper lip, where small amounts of filler can add volume and subtly elongate the lip. In some cases, careful placement in the subnasal area can provide additional structural support. A thorough understanding of vascular anatomy, particularly the course of the angular artery, is crucial to avoid complications, and proper injection technique including slow delivery and aspiration reduces the risk of intravascular injection.

**Combining Modalities**

This technique is sometimes combined with neuromodulators such as Botox for individuals with hypermobile upper lips — Botox reduces the elevation of the lip during smiling while filler adds volume and structure. However, fillers alone are often sufficient for patients whose main concern is lip thinness or lack of gum coverage.

A common mistake injectors make is not carrying out a full assessment. Botox should generally be considered first, and lip filler can then be considered if the desired result has not been achieved.

**Contraindications**

Before treatment, discuss the patient's medical history and allergies. Those who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or have certain health conditions may be advised against the procedure. Individuals with a history of severe allergic reactions are also not suitable candidates.

**Filler Selection**

It's important to note that some filler types are not recommended as they can produce lumpy results. Hyaluronic acid fillers are generally preferred as they are reversible.

**Managing Expectations**

A comprehensive assessment by a trained provider should determine the underlying cause of the gummy smile and whether lip fillers alone — or in combination with other treatments — can achieve the desired outcome. Results typically last 6 to 12 months depending on the individual's metabolism and filler product used.

In summary, thorough assessment of the etiology is the most critical step — filler works best as part of a considered treatment plan, often alongside Botox, rather than as a standalone first-line approach.

Here's a breakdown of the risks associated with InMode treatments, organised by frequency and severity. Note that InMode covers multiple technologies (Morpheus8, FaceTite, BodyTite, AccuTite, Forma, Lumecca, etc.), so risks can vary by modality.

---

**Common / Expected Side Effects**

These are usually mild and temporary: redness, swelling, warmth, and tenderness in the treated area are standard. Bruising is possible, especially with RFAL procedures like BodyTite/FaceTite or deeper Morpheus8 treatments. For Morpheus8 specifically, pinpoint bleeding, temporary grid marks from the needles, and small scabs during healing can occur. With Lumecca IPL, pigmented spots may temporarily darken before flaking off.

---

**Less Common but Notable Risks**

Less commonly, risks include burns, blisters, infection, scarring, prolonged pain, or changes in pigmentation — either hyperpigmentation (darker spots) or hypopigmentation (lighter spots). Following pre- and post-care instructions is crucial to minimise these.

Rarely, there may be temporary or more lasting changes in skin texture.

---

**Serious / Rare Risks**

FDA adverse event reports (MAUDE) have documented more serious outcomes including unwanted permanent fat loss, structural changes to facial contour requiring surgical repair, and nerve damage — for example, facial sagging following FaceTite. Burns from BodyTite have also been reported.

Nerve damage, while very rare, is a recognised risk specifically with FaceTite and AccuTite procedures.

---

**Skin Type Considerations**

Morpheus8 is generally considered suitable for all Fitzpatrick skin types (I–VI), as insulated needles help minimise the risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation. Serious complications are rare, estimated at 1–5%, and most resolve without intervention.

---

**Contraindications**

Patients who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or have certain underlying health conditions may not be suitable candidates. Medical history and allergies should always be discussed thoroughly before treatment. Active skin infections, metal implants in the treatment area, pacemakers, and a history of keloid scarring are also widely recognised contraindications.

---

**Key Takeaway for Practitioners**

The overall safety profile is favourable for a well-selected patient with a trained operator. However, the RFAL-based devices (FaceTite, BodyTite, AccuTite) carry higher risk than non-invasive options like Forma or Lumecca, given their minimally invasive nature and the heat delivered subdermally. Thorough assessment, correct energy settings, and real-time temperature monitoring are critical to avoiding thermal injury.

Here's a summary of Rejuran's history and the state of the evidence:

---

**Origins & Timeline**

Rejuran was first developed in South Korea, a country globally recognised for its leadership in cosmetic dermatology and regenerative medicine. Scientists researching wound healing were particularly interested in polynucleotides (PN) — long-chain molecules that make up DNA — and found that when extracted and purified from salmon sperm cells, these molecules showed exceptional biocompatibility with human skin.

In 2014, the first PN to be used as a skin booster was Rejuran®. It was first approved in Singapore in 2017, introduced as a unique concept of skin rejuvenation by administering polynucleotides into the dermis. In late 2018, the product line expanded to include Rejuran-I (eye area), Rejuran-S (acne scars), and Rejuran-HB (intensive hydration).

Originally developed in South Korea and now embraced by elite dermatologists, Rejuran has been used in injectable form overseas for years. While injectables are not currently FDA-cleared for use in the US, it is gaining popularity stateside in serum form paired with microneedling or laser treatments.

---

**The Research Landscape**

The landmark study was a Phase III randomised controlled trial. In this clinical trial, 72 patients were randomised to receive Rejuran® treatment for crow's feet on one side and Yvoire-Hydro® (a hyaluronic acid filler) on the other, with repeated treatments every two weeks for a total of three times over 12 weeks. In safety outcomes, there were no unexpected adverse effects, and the data suggested Rejuran® as a new regenerative filler could be useful for wrinkle reduction.

In a randomised split-face clinical trial involving 27 participants, Rejuran® was compared with an HA filler. The PDRN-treated side showed significantly higher Global Aesthetic Improvement Scale (GAIS) scores at 16 weeks and a greater reduction in skin roughness by week 28. In another study involving 218 Asian subjects, Rejuran® led to a 61.4% reduction in periorbital wrinkles after 12 weeks, with adverse events limited to minor swelling, pain, bruising, and erythema.

In a clinical trial involving 150 patients, researchers noted that PDRN not only improved visible signs of aging but also enhanced the skin's overall health markers including hydration, barrier function, and cellular turnover. PDRN has also been clinically proven to speed up wound healing, helping repair skin up to 92% faster than untreated wounds.

---

**Overall Assessment of the Evidence**

PDRN/PN formulations such as Rejuran® are biologically plausible regenerative injectables with supportive preclinical and early clinical evidence for wound healing and aesthetic rejuvenation. Current data indicate meaningful short-term benefits with a favourable safety profile, but lack of standardisation and long-term evidence warrant cautious integration into practice. Large, rigorously designed RCTs remain a priority.

The overall evidence base is made up largely of small trials, and effect sizes and durability vary between studies. An honest summary is that the mechanism is biologically plausible and short-term skin-quality benefits are reported, but high-quality, large, long-term trials remain limited.

In short, Rejuran has a solid biological rationale and growing clinical support, but the field still needs larger, standardised, longer-term trials to fully establish its durability and comparative effectiveness.

Here's a clear breakdown of the differences between the two treatments, along with their Korean terminology:

---

**Korean Names**

- **Lip Flip** → **립플립** (rip peullip) or **입꼬리 보톡스** (ip-kkori botokseu — "lip corner Botox")
- **Gummy Smile Treatment** → **거미스마일 교정** (geomi seumailgyo-jeong) or **잇몸 보톡스** (it-mom botokseu — "gum Botox")

---

**The Key Differences**

| | Lip Flip (립플립) | Gummy Smile (거미스마일 보톡스) |
|---|---|---|
| **Goal** | Make upper lip appear fuller/more visible | Reduce gum exposure when smiling |
| **Target muscle** | Orbicularis oris (around the lip border) | Levator labii superioris alaeque nasi (LLSAN) — lifts the upper lip |
| **Injection site** | Along the vermilion border / cupid's bow and lip corners | Just below the nose, at the base of the LLSAN where it inserts near the nasal alar |
| **Mechanism** | Relaxes the muscle that curls the lip inward, causing it to roll outward | Weakens the muscle that pulls the upper lip upward excessively when smiling |
| **Who it's for** | Thin or "disappearing" upper lip when smiling | Patients where 3mm or more of gum is visible on smiling |

---

**How They Overlap — and Where People Get Confused**

Both treatments use Botox, but the goals and results are different. The Botox Lip Flip enhances the appearance of your upper lip by flipping it outward, while Gummy Smile Correction reduces visible gum when smiling.

A lip flip uses small amounts of Botox injected near the upper lip to relax the muscles that pull the lip inward when you smile, allowing the lip to "flip" slightly outward — creating a fuller, more defined look without adding volume. Gummy smile correction targets specific muscles around the upper lip to relax them, preventing the lip from rising too high when smiling.

The confusion arises because both can incidentally help a mildly gummy smile. However, the primary muscle targeted in gummy smile treatment is the levator labii superioris alaeque nasi (LLSAN) — which is overactive in gummy smile patients, causing the upper lip to lift excessively and reveal more gum tissue. The lip flip targets a different muscle entirely (orbicularis oris) for a different purpose.

In Korean aesthetic clinics, lip corner Botox (립플립) falls under the "gummy smile / nasal flare / lip corner" category, which is why the two are often grouped together — but they address different concerns.

Many practitioners offer them in combination for a comprehensive smile refresh.

Here's a thorough comparison:

---

**Background**

Botox (onabotulinumtoxinA, manufactured by Allergan/AbbVie) has been FDA-approved for cosmetic use since 2002 and has the longest track record of any neuromodulator on the US market. Xeomin (incobotulinumtoxinA, manufactured by Merz Aesthetics) received FDA cosmetic approval in 2011, specifically for glabellar lines.

---

**The Core Difference: Protein Load**

Botox contains accessory proteins, while Xeomin is a purified version without them. Xeomin contains significantly less clostridial protein (approximately 0.6 ng per 100 units) compared to Botox (approximately 5 ng per 100 units). This is why Xeomin is often called the "naked injectable" — it contains only the active botulinum toxin molecule with nothing else attached.

---

**Antibody Resistance**

The reduced protein load with Xeomin may decrease the risk of neutralising antibody development. In clinical studies of Xeomin including 2,649 patients, only 0.3% were positive for neutralising antibodies after treatment. The risk of developing antibodies appears to correlate with the total protein concentration injected per treatment session.

In cases of suspected resistance, switching to Xeomin's protein-free formulation is a reasonable clinical step, as any antibodies formed against the accessory proteins in Botox would be irrelevant to Xeomin's pure toxin formulation.

---

**Onset & Duration**

Both generally take 3–5 days to begin working, with full effect visible at 2 weeks. Xeomin has quicker results and possibly fewer allergic reactions. Duration for both is typically 3–4 months, with no clinically significant difference between them.

---

**Dosing & Spread**

Xeomin and Botox share a 1:1 unit equivalence and a similar spread pattern, so they perform comparably across most treatment areas. Some providers observe that Xeomin stays relatively localised, making it preferable for smaller, more precise treatment areas, while others favour Botox for larger muscle groups where predictability matters. These preferences are based on individual clinical experience rather than significant pharmacological differences.

---

**Storage**

One practical distinction: Xeomin does not require refrigeration before reconstitution, whereas Botox does. This can be advantageous for clinics with storage constraints.

---

**FDA-Approved Indications**

While both contain botulinum toxin type A, Botox has a longer track record with more FDA-approved uses and extensive clinical research supporting its safety and effectiveness. There are no specific conditions that Xeomin treats that Botox cannot.

---

**Clinical Efficacy**

A study in the Journal of Neurological Sciences found Xeomin showed "non-inferiority" to Botox when used in the same doses, concluding it is a safe and effective treatment.

---

**In Summary**

| | Botox | Xeomin |
|---|---|---|
| Manufacturer | Allergan/AbbVie | Merz Aesthetics |
| Proteins | Active toxin + accessory proteins | Active toxin only ("naked") |
| FDA cosmetic approval | 2002 | 2011 |
| Unit equivalence | — | 1:1 with Botox |
| Antibody risk | Higher (more protein) | Lower |
| Storage | Refrigeration required | Room temp before reconstitution |
| Track record | More extensive | Growing evidence base |
| Best for | Broad treatment, established patients | Long-term users, protein-sensitive patients, precise areas |

For most patients, either product delivers comparable results. Xeomin is worth considering particularly for patients who feel their Botox results have been diminishing over time, which can be a sign of antibody development.

Does lip flip consist of Botox AND filler?? Or just one specifically

### 🤖 **Claude**

The lip flip uses **only Botox (neurotoxin)** — no filler involved.

A lip flip uses a small, precise amount of neuromodulator injected along the upper lip border. By relaxing the orbicularis oris muscle, the upper lip gently rolls outward, making it appear more visible and defined **without adding any volume**.

A lip flip uses Botox to relax the upper lip muscle, while lip fillers use hyaluronic acid to add volume directly to the lips. Lip flips offer a subtle, natural look by everting the upper lip without increasing actual volume.

The confusion often comes because some clinics offer them as a combined package — for example, some Korean clinics offer a "Lip Design Filler" package which includes filler plus lip corner Botox together — but these are two separate treatments being bundled, not one procedure. The lip flip itself is purely neurotoxin.
