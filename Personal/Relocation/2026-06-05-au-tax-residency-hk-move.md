---

## 1. Ceasing Australian Tax Residency — The Key First Step

Sarah is currently taxed as an **Australian tax resident**, meaning she pays Australian tax on her **worldwide income**. Moving to Hong Kong doesn't automatically change this.

Australian tax residency for 2024 and 2025 tax returns is still determined by four existing tests under the Income Tax Assessment Act 1936: the **Resides Test** (based on intention, physical presence, family and business ties), the **Domicile Test** (you're a resident if your domicile is in Australia unless your "permanent place of abode" is overseas), the **183-Day Test**, and the Commonwealth Superannuation Test.

To stop being taxed as an Australian resident, she needs to show the ATO that her "centre of life" has shifted abroad. Simply working in Hong Kong isn't enough — the ATO will look at where her home, finances, and family are based. Retaining her investment properties in Australia, Australian bank accounts, and close family ties in Australia could cause the ATO to continue treating her as a tax resident.

**Important upcoming change:** The Government has announced an intention to move to a "Bright Line" test (where 183 days of physical presence automatically triggers residency) and a "45-Day Rule" for complex cases. These changes are slated for future implementation, likely affecting income years commencing 1 July 2026, subject to final legislation.

---

## 2. No Double Tax Agreement Between Australia and Hong Kong

This is a critical and often-missed point. There is currently no DTA between Australia and Hong Kong, meaning Australian expats there must rely on domestic relief provisions rather than treaty protections.

*Note: One source suggests a treaty may have been agreed in principle (with reduced withholding rates cited), but as of mid-2026 this does not appear to be in force. Sarah should verify the current status with a tax adviser.*

The absence of a DTA means:
- There is **no treaty mechanism** to cap Australian withholding taxes on dividends or interest at reduced rates
- There is **no formal relief** to prevent the same income being taxed in both Australia and Hong Kong
- Sarah must rely on **domestic unilateral foreign tax credit** provisions in each country

Hong Kong, however, only taxes income **sourced in Hong Kong**, so her Australian-sourced income (rent, dividends) generally won't be taxed by Hong Kong at all — limiting the double taxation risk in practice.

---

## 3. Once She Becomes a Non-Resident: Australian Tax Obligations

Assuming Sarah successfully ceases Australian tax residency, here's what happens to each asset class:

### 🏘 Investment Properties — Rental Income
Non-residents are taxed only on Australian-sourced income and cannot claim the tax-free threshold. Rental income from her two Australian properties will remain **fully taxable in Australia** as Australian-sourced income. She must still lodge an Australian tax return each year and will be taxed at **non-resident marginal rates starting at 32.5%** from the first dollar, with no tax-free threshold.

On the positive side, non-residents do not pay the Medicare levy of 2%.

### 🏘 Investment Properties — Capital Gains (if she sells)
Net capital gains are calculated in the same way for non-residents as for residents, except the **50% CGT discount is not available** for capital gains accrued after 8 May 2012. This is a significant disadvantage — as a resident she'd normally halve her taxable gain on assets held over 12 months; as a non-resident she cannot.

From 1 January 2025, significant changes to Australia's Foreign Resident Capital Gains Withholding regime apply — there is now a **15% withholding rate** (up from 12.5%) and the previous $750,000 property price threshold has been removed, meaning all property sales by non-residents are subject to withholding. This is a cash flow issue at settlement (not extra tax per se, as it's credited against her actual liability when she lodges).

### 📈 Share Investment Portfolio
The standard non-resident withholding tax rate on unfranked dividends is 30%, reducible under a double tax agreement. Franking credits cannot be refunded to non-residents — so the company tax already paid loses its value to her. For **fully franked dividends**, these are usually exempt from withholding tax, which is a silver lining.

For capital gains on **Australian shares**, the position depends on whether the shares are considered "Taxable Australian Property." Ordinary listed shares in Australian companies are generally **not** TAP (unlike property), so capital gains on selling them as a non-resident are typically **not subject to Australian CGT** — though she should confirm this with an adviser given her specific portfolio.

### 💼 Hong Kong Employment Income
Hong Kong only taxes income sourced in Hong Kong. Her HK salary will be taxed in Hong Kong at relatively low rates (top rate around 17%). Since she'll be a non-resident of Australia, her Hong Kong salary will **not** be taxable in Australia.

---

## 4. The "CGT Departure Event" — Critical

When Sarah **ceases to be an Australian tax resident**, this triggers a deemed **CGT event** on most of her assets. Assets subject to this departure tax include non-Australian property assets (like her share portfolio) — the ATO treats her as if she sold and immediately re-acquired them at market value on the date she ceases residency. She can elect to defer this, but it will crystallise eventually.

Her **Australian investment properties** are exempt from this departure CGT event (they remain taxable Australian property regardless), but her **share portfolio** could trigger a substantial deemed gain when she leaves.

Great follow-up question. The answer is nuanced — there are scenarios where **remaining an Australian tax resident could actually be advantageous**, despite the intuitive assumption that "leaving" the tax net is always better.

---

## The Core Trade-off

As an **Australian tax resident**, Sarah pays Australian tax on **worldwide income** — including her Hong Kong salary. But she retains valuable concessions. As a **non-resident**, she escapes Australian tax on her HK salary, but loses significant benefits on her Australian assets.

Here's how it plays out across each area:

---

## 1. 🏘 Investment Properties

**Remaining a resident is better here.**

As a resident she retains the **50% CGT discount** on any future sale (for assets held 12+ months). As established above, non-residents lose this entirely for the post-May 2012 accrual period. On a property with a $500,000 gain, that's a difference of $250,000 of additional taxable income as a non-resident — potentially $80,000–$100,000+ in extra tax.

Rental income is taxable either way, but as a resident she can offset it against other deductions, losses, and the tax-free threshold.

---

## 2. 📈 Share Portfolio

**Remaining a resident is better here too.**

- She keeps the **50% CGT discount** on share gains
- She can **use franking credits** to offset her tax liability (and potentially receive refunds)
- No departure CGT event is triggered
- No withholding tax complications

---

## 3. 💼 Hong Kong Salary — The Big Cost of Staying a Resident

**This is where remaining a resident hurts.**

As an Australian resident, her **full HK salary is taxable in Australia** at Australian marginal rates (up to 45% + 2% Medicare levy for high earners). She would receive a **foreign income tax offset (FITO)** for Hong Kong tax paid, but:

- Hong Kong's top tax rate is ~**17%** versus Australia's top rate of **47%**
- The FITO only offsets the HK tax paid — she'd still pay the **difference** to Australia
- On a HK salary of, say, $300,000 AUD equivalent, the **"top-up" tax payable to Australia could be $60,000–$90,000+ per year**

This is the dominant financial cost of remaining a resident, and it grows the higher her HK salary is.

---

## 4. 🏥 Medicare Levy

As a resident she continues to pay the **2% Medicare levy** — despite living in Hong Kong and getting no benefit from Australia's health system. She may be able to apply for a Medicare levy exemption as a resident living overseas, but this requires specific conditions to be met.

---

## The Break-Even Analysis

The right answer depends largely on **two variables**:

| Factor | Favours Remaining Resident | Favours Becoming Non-Resident |
|---|---|---|
| **HK salary is high** | ❌ Big Australian top-up tax | ✅ HK salary untaxed in Aus |
| **Investment properties likely to be sold while overseas** | ✅ Keeps 50% CGT discount | ❌ Loses CGT discount |
| **Share portfolio large with embedded gains** | ✅ No departure CGT event | ❌ Deemed disposal on exit |
| **Heavy franking credits on shares** | ✅ Can fully utilise credits | ❌ Credits wasted |
| **Staying in HK short-term (1–2 years)** | ✅ Simpler, no need to sever ties | ❌ Not worth restructuring |
| **Staying in HK long-term (5+ years)** | ❌ Years of top-up tax adds up | ✅ HK salary savings compound |

---

## A Practical Scenario

Assume Sarah earns **AUD $250,000** in Hong Kong and has investment properties with **$800,000 in combined embedded capital gains**.

**As a non-resident:**
- Saves ~$70,000/year in Australian tax on HK salary
- But when she sells properties, loses the 50% CGT discount → extra tax on $400,000 of gains → roughly **$130,000–$160,000 in additional CGT**
- If she stays 3 years, salary savings = ~$210,000, but CGT cost = $130,000–$160,000 — marginal benefit only

**As a resident:**
- Pays top-up tax each year on HK salary (~$70,000/year)
- But retains CGT discount → saves $130,000–$160,000 on eventual property sale
- If she only stays 2–3 years, this could be a **wash or even better** to remain a resident

---

## The Bottom Line

**For a short stay (under ~3 years):** Remaining an Australian tax resident is often financially **comparable or better**, especially if she has significant unrealised CGT in her property or share portfolio. The CGT discount alone can outweigh years of HK salary top-up tax.

**For a long stay (5+ years):** The accumulating top-up tax on a high HK salary likely tips the scales toward **ceasing residency** — but only if she carefully manages the departure CGT event on her shares and doesn't sell the properties while a non-resident.

**The ideal strategy** is often a hybrid: remain a resident initially, **sell or restructure key assets before departing** (to lock in the CGT discount), and then formally cease residency if the HK stay extends beyond 3–4 years.

Yes — but with some important nuances around **how** the 50% CGT discount applies to foreign shares.

---

## The Short Answer

**Yes, the 50% CGT discount applies to US shares** held by an Australian tax resident for more than 12 months, just as it does for Australian shares. The discount is based on **residency status and holding period** — not where the shares are listed or domiciled.

---

## How It Works

When Sarah sells her US shares after 13 months:

1. **Calculate the capital gain** — sale proceeds minus cost base (converted to AUD at the relevant exchange rates)
2. **Apply the 50% CGT discount** — halve the gain
3. **Add the discounted gain to her assessable income** — taxed at her marginal rate

So if she made a $100,000 gain on US shares, only $50,000 is included in her taxable income.

---

## The Foreign Currency Complication

This is where US shares differ from Australian ones. **All calculations must be in AUD**, which introduces a second layer of gain or loss:

- The **cost base** is the AUD equivalent of what she paid (using the AUD/USD rate at purchase date)
- The **proceeds** are the AUD equivalent of what she received (using the AUD/USD rate at sale date)
- If the AUD weakened against the USD during the holding period, her AUD gain is **amplified** even if the share price didn't move much — and vice versa
- This **foreign currency gain/loss is rolled into the same CGT calculation** — it is not treated separately

So the 50% discount applies to the **total AUD gain including the currency movement**, which can work for or against her.

---

## The US Tax Angle — Potential Double Taxation

Because she is an Australian tax resident earning income from US assets, the **Australia-US Double Tax Agreement (DTA) is relevant**:

- The US may also seek to tax the capital gain, particularly if she has US tax filing obligations (e.g. if she is also a US person, which she is not based on the facts)
- As an **Australian resident with no US person status**, the US generally **does not tax non-US persons on capital gains from selling US listed shares** — so double taxation is typically **not an issue** for Sarah specifically
- She would declare and pay CGT in Australia only, with the 50% discount applied

---

## What She Does NOT Get

| Benefit | Available? |
|---|---|
| 50% CGT discount (held 13 months) | ✅ Yes |
| Foreign currency gain folded into CGT calc | ✅ Yes (can help or hurt) |
| Franking credits on US dividends | ❌ No — US companies don't frank dividends |
| Foreign tax offset for US withholding on dividends | ✅ Yes — 15% US withholding on dividends can be offset against Australian tax |
| CGT discount if she later becomes a non-resident | ❌ Lost for gains accrued during non-residency |

---

## Key Takeaway

The 50% CGT discount **does apply** to Sarah's US shares held over 12 months — her 13-month holding period qualifies comfortably. The main complexity versus Australian shares is the **AUD/USD conversion** affecting her true gain, and ensuring she correctly converts both the cost base and proceeds using the ATO's accepted exchange rates. A tax accountant would typically use the **RBA exchange rate** on each relevant date.

> ⚠️ *General information only — not personal tax advice. Exchange rate calculations and foreign income reporting for US shares can be fiddly; a tax agent familiar with foreign investments is worth engaging at lodgement time.*

## Part 1: Selling as a Hong Kong Tax Resident (Non-Resident of Australia)

### What Happens to the Shares on Departure?

First, a critical point that happens **before** you even sell:

When you cease to be an Australian tax resident, the ATO treats you as having **disposed of and immediately reacquired** your shares at market value on the day you cease residency. This is called a **CGT departure event (CGT event I1)**.

You have two choices at that point:

| Election | What Happens |
|---|---|
| **No election (default)** | Deemed disposal triggers immediately — CGT is payable in the year you depart, based on the gain to that date |
| **Elect to defer** | No tax on departure; instead the shares become "Taxable Australian Property" and CGT is deferred until actual sale |

Most people **elect to defer**, but this comes with a significant catch (explained below).

---

### If You Defer and Sell as a Hong Kong Resident

Once you've elected to defer and you later sell the shares from Hong Kong:

**1. The shares are treated as Taxable Australian Property (TAP)**
This means Australia **retains the right to tax the gain** — even though you're a Hong Kong resident. You will still owe Australian CGT.

**2. No 50% CGT discount**
This is the critical loss. The 50% CGT discount is not available for capital gains accrued after 8 May 2012 for non-residents. The ATO will apportion the gain:
- Gain accrued **while you were an Australian resident** → eligible for 50% discount
- Gain accrued **after you became a non-resident** → no discount, fully taxable

**3. Taxed at non-resident rates**
A non-resident individual is liable to Australian income tax only on Australian-sourced income and certain statutory income including certain capital gains. Non-resident rates start at 32.5% from the first dollar — no tax-free threshold.

**4. Hong Kong will NOT tax the gain**
Hong Kong does not impose capital gains tax. So there is no double taxation issue on the gain itself — but you also get no HK credit to offset against the Australian liability.

**5. No DTA protection**
There is currently no DTA between Australia and Hong Kong, so there is no treaty mechanism to reduce or eliminate the Australian CGT obligation.

---

## Part 2: The Comparison — Sell as Australian Resident vs. Hong Kong Resident

Let's model this with a concrete example to make the comparison tangible.

**Assumptions:**
- Shares purchased for **AUD $200,000** (cost base)
- Current market value when you depart: **AUD $500,000** (gain to date: $300,000)
- Projected value when sold in future: **AUD $700,000** (total gain: $500,000)
- Marginal tax rate as Australian resident: **47%** (45% + 2% Medicare)
- Non-resident marginal rate: **45%** (no Medicare levy)
- Held for more than 12 months in all scenarios

---

### Scenario A: Sell Before Departing (as Australian Tax Resident)

| | |
|---|---|
| Total gain | $500,000 |
| After 50% CGT discount | $250,000 taxable |
| Tax at 47% marginal rate | **~$117,500** |
| Net proceeds retained | **~$582,500** |

✅ Full 50% discount applies
✅ Simple, clean — no ongoing Australian filing obligation for shares
❌ You crystallise the gain now, losing potential future growth untaxed

### Scenario B: Sell After Departing (as Hong Kong Resident, shares become TAP)

The gain must be **apportioned** between the resident and non-resident periods:

| Period | Gain | Discount | Taxable Amount |
|---|---|---|---|
| While Australian resident (to departure) | $300,000 | 50% discount | $150,000 |
| While non-resident (post-departure growth) | $200,000 | No discount | $200,000 |

❌ $40,000 more tax than Scenario A
❌ Must still lodge Australian tax returns
❌ 15% withholding on sale proceeds upfront (refunded/credited on lodgement)
✅ No HK capital gains tax
✅ Post-departure growth deferred — you had use of that capital longer

---

### Scenario C: Return to Australia in 10 Years, Then Sell

If you return and re-establish Australian tax residency before selling:

- You are treated as **re-acquiring the shares at market value on the date you return**
- Gains accrued **during your non-residency period** are effectively **reset** at the re-entry cost base
- You then hold them as a resident and get the 50% discount again on future gains
- **The non-resident period gain is essentially forgiven** for CGT purposes

This is actually a **highly advantageous outcome** if you plan to return:

| | |
|---|---|
| Value when you return (estimated) | $700,000 |
| New cost base on return | $700,000 |
| Future gains after return | Eligible for 50% discount |
| Tax on gains during non-residency | **$0** |

✅ Non-residency period gain permanently escapes Australian CGT
✅ Clock resets on return — full 50% discount available again
⚠️ Requires you to **actually return** and re-establish residency before selling

---

## Summary: Which Is Better?

| Scenario | Tax Paid | Best If... |
|---|---|---|
| **Sell before departing** | ~$117,500 | You want simplicity and certainty now |
| **Sell while in HK** | ~$157,500 | You need the capital while overseas |
| **Return, then sell** | Potentially **~$0 on HK-period gains** | You genuinely plan to return to Australia |

---

## The Strategic Takeaway

The **"return in 10 years then sell" scenario is financially the most powerful** — but only if the return is genuine. The ATO scrutinises residency re-establishment and will challenge it if they believe it's artificial.

If return is uncertain, **selling before you depart** as an Australian resident is almost always better than selling from Hong Kong — the $40,000 difference in the example above would grow significantly with larger portfolios.

The worst outcome is **selling from Hong Kong without planning** — you lose the 50% discount on post-departure gains, pay at non-resident rates, and have no DTA protection.
